\begin{abstract}
Speech-to-speech (S2S) systems can produce fluent spoken responses, but fluency alone does not guarantee conversational naturalness.
A response may be intelligible and semantically appropriate while sounding atypical in timing, rhythm, pitch variation, or affect for the conversation that preceded it.
We present a conversation-conditioned modeling framework for distributions over speech-native response measurements in natural dyadic interaction.
The method combines interpretable speech and context features, optional learned speech embeddings, recent conversational history, and a history-aware CVAE over response behavior measurements.
We analyze held-out natural conversations and an external EmotiVoice-derived synthetic probe, using posterior conditional fit scores, likelihood-gap summaries, and listener ratings of the probe conditions.
The experiments show that directly measured conversation features are strongest for fitting held-out natural response measurements, while learned speech embeddings provide complementary signal for separating natural responses from the synthetic probe.
These findings support a two-axis analysis of S2S naturalness: modeling measurable natural conversational behavior and testing sensitivity to perceptually unnatural speech conditions.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Spoken conversation is organized through more than lexical content.
Speakers coordinate turns using timing, prosody, pause structure, and affective stance, and these cues help make an exchange sound smooth rather than merely intelligible \cite{riest_anticipation_2015, levinson_turn-taking_2016, koiso_analysis_1998}.
S2S systems are increasingly able to generate fluent speech, but generated turns can still violate the behavioral ranges that are typical for a given conversational context.
For example, a response may compress pitch variation in an expressive exchange, or speak at an implausible rate, or even use affective cues that do not match the preceding turn.
Text-based metrics, task success, and global listener ratings remain useful, but they often do not identify which speech-native dimensions make an output sound unnatural \cite{omahony_combining_2022}.

Prior work on reference-based prosody and rhythm evaluation for spoken dialogue systems introduced a complementary view: extract prosodic and rhythmic measurements from an S2S output, compare them with matched human conversational regimes, and report interpretable deviations rather than a single opaque score \cite{DISTRIB}.
That approach is intentionally simple, but it treats reference regimes as static tables.
The present paper asks a complementary question: can we learn a conditional distribution over response behavior from the immediate preceding turn and recent dialogue history?
Instead of asking whether an output falls inside a broad matched percentile range, we ask whether its response measurements are likely under a distribution expected for this particular conversational context.

We use the term \emph{conditioning turn} for the preceding speaker turn and \emph{response turn} for the next cross-speaker turn.
Together they form an \emph{adjacent-turn pair}.
The conditioning turn is the actual speech segment that came immediately before the response in a dyadic conversation.
For each pair, we extract interpretable speech measurements from both sides, optionally add learned speech embeddings from the conditioning turn, and model the response-side measurements.
The core object is
\begin{equation}
    p_{\theta}(y_t \mid x_t, h_t^{(H)}),
    \label{eq:conditional_distribution}
\end{equation}
where $x_t$ describes the current conditioning turn and observed turn-timing covariates, $h_t^{(H)}$ contains up to $H$ preceding turns from the same interaction, and $y_t$ is a vector of response-side speech measurements.

This paper makes five contributions.
First, we convert Seamless Interaction conversations into adjacent-turn examples for distributional S2S evaluation.
Second, we define a 12-measure response behavior space covering duration, lexical length, pitch variation, voicing, rate, pausing, and dimensional affect.
Third, we compare interpretable-only and embedding-augmented history-aware CVAE models over history windows $H\in\{1,3,5,7\}$\footnote{The code will be made publicly available upon acceptance.}.
Fourth, we evaluate each model on two axes: held-out natural-response fit and likelihood separation between natural responses and a TRACE-derived EmotiVoice probe \cite{TRACE}.
Fifth, we report listener ratings showing that the TRACE-derived EmotiVoice probe is perceptually less natural than natural/reference speech.

\section{Related Work}
\subsection{Speech-Native S2S Evaluation}
S2S evaluation has inherited many tools from automatic speech recognition, text generation, and text-to-speech evaluation.
These methods measure important aspects of output quality, but they can miss conversational timing, turn-taking fit, prosodic expressivity, and context-appropriate speaking behavior.
Subjective ratings are essential, yet they are expensive and often provide limited feature-level detail.
Recent studies of spoken dialogue systems show that speech rate, prosody, and interaction partner behavior shape user experience and conversational dynamics, reinforcing the need for evaluation signals that remain in the speech domain \cite{dowding_user_2024, xie_speaking_2024, jung_comparison_2025}.
Our goal is not to replace human evaluation, but to provide a scalable analysis that identifies which measurable dimensions of a response deviate from natural conversational behavior.

\subsection{Conversational Prosody and Timing}
Conversational speech differs from read speech in prosodic and temporal structure \cite{howell1991comparison, hazan2010does}.
Prosodic variation, especially fundamental-frequency ($F_0$) range and variability, is associated with emotion, communicative intent, prominence, and discourse structure \cite{banziger_role_2005, pell_emotional_2011, xu_speech_2005, sluijter_acoustic_1996, sluijter_spectral_1996}.
Temporal organization also matters: speaking rate, articulation rate, and pausing shape perceived tempo and turn-taking behavior \cite{tauroza_speech_1990, goldman1958speech, campione2002large, pellegrino_automatic_2004, jiao_estimating_2015, morgan_combining_1998}.
Because these behaviors vary with interactional state, S2S analysis should compare a response with expectations for its local conversation, not with a single global baseline \cite{fusaroli_dialog_2014, wynn_conversational_2024}.

\subsection{Natural Adjacent-Turn Examples}
We construct natural examples from Seamless Interaction, a large dyadic English conversation corpus containing naturalistic and improvised face-to-face interactions \cite{seamless_interaction}.
Word-level transcript events from the same speaker are merged when separated by at most 1.0 s, and VAD regions are merged with the same threshold.
Transcript boundaries are refined against overlapping VAD regions or VAD regions within a 0.5 s tolerance.
We retain adjacent turns only when the speaker changes, remove short backchannel-only turns, and keep conditioning and response segments between 3 and 15 s.
Pairs with more than 0.25 s of overlap or more than 1.0 s of response latency are excluded.

The refined experiments use the source dataset partitions directly: Seamless train/dev examples are used for training, and Seamless test examples are held out for natural-response evaluation.
After filtering and embedding alignment, the reported experiments contain 85,566 natural training examples and 1,146 held-out natural examples.

\subsection{TRACE-Derived EmotiVoice Probe}
To test whether models trained on natural responses assign lower fit scores to synthetic speech conditions, we use EmotiVoice-derived conditions from TRACE, a temporal relationship-aware conversational entrainment study, as an external probe \cite{TRACE}.
That study constructs synthetic and voice-converted dyadic clips that are intended to disrupt natural conversational entrainment.
Here, we do not treat this material as a general benchmark dataset; we use its aligned EmotiVoice examples as a deliberately unnatural stress test.
The same adjacent-turn construction and measurement pipeline is applied, producing examples with the same fields as the natural Seamless examples.
The raw probe table contains 3,731 candidate rows; 2,645 remain after filtering and embedding alignment.

\subsection{Response Measurements}
The response space reported in this paper contains 12 speech-native measurements adapted from reference-based prosody and rhythm evaluation for spoken dialogue systems and extended with dimensional affect estimates \cite{DISTRIB}.
Table~\ref{tab:target_metrics} defines each measurement and explains why it is a candidate dimension for S2S naturalness analysis.
These measurements are not naturalness labels; they are observable response behaviors that can make a generated turn sound more or less plausible in context.
We intentionally exclude automatic-analysis outputs that are not response behavior measurements for S2S agents, including demographic-style estimates and analysis-coverage variables.
Those quantities may be useful for quality control or exploratory analysis, but they should not determine whether a synthetic response is considered conversationally natural.
The broader training run included auxiliary demographic and coverage outputs used by the feature pipeline; all aggregate results, model comparisons, and per-feature claims in this paper are restricted to the 12 measurements in Table~\ref{tab:target_metrics}.

Pitch is estimated with Praat's autocorrelation tracker through Parselmouth using a 75--500 Hz range \cite{jadoul2018introducing, vogel2009standardization}.
Only voiced frames are used for $F_0$ statistics, and the 10th--90th percentile interval is used to reduce the effect of tracking outliers.
Pauses are inter-word gaps of at least 0.2 s, a common threshold for distinguishing conversational pauses from shorter closures or alignment noise \cite{campione2002large}.
The use of words per minute is motivated by its relationship to perceived speech tempo in spontaneous speech \cite{iwarsson2023measuring}.
Arousal, valence, and dominance are produced by VoxProfile's speech-based affect model after mono conversion, 16 kHz resampling, RMS normalization, and speech-region selection with Silero VAD \cite{Silero_VAD, feng2025vox}.

\subsection{Conditioning Features and History}
For each current pair, $x_t$ contains measurements from the conditioning turn, relationship and turn-position context, observed response-timing covariates (latency and overlap), and optional conditioning-turn speech embeddings.
The timing covariates define the scoring context for these experiments; because they are conditioned on and heavily filtered, the reported models do not evaluate unusual delays, interruptions, or overlap as response failures.
The response measurements form $y_t$, the object whose distribution is analyzed.
In a deployable prior-predictive use, $y_t$ would be scored only after sampling from the prior conditioned on $x_t$ and history; in the current posterior-fit analysis, $y_t$ also enters the inference network used to estimate the latent variable.
The history vector $h_t^{(H)}$ is a padded sequence of up to $H$ earlier turns from the same interaction, ordered from oldest to newest.
Each history item contains the earlier turn's interpretable features, optional embeddings, and the earlier response measurements that would already be observable in a multi-turn interaction.

\subsection{Modeling Rationale}
The model needs to represent a range of plausible response behaviors rather than a single canonical response, because many prosodic, rate, pausing, and affective patterns can be natural in the same conversation context.
We therefore use a CVAE to model residual response variability with a latent variable while conditioning on the current turn and recent interaction history \cite{kingma2013vae, sohn2015cvae}.
We compare interpretable-only and embedding-augmented conditions because the former are easier to interpret, while learned speech embeddings may retain acoustic or paralinguistic information that is not captured by the 12 response metrics.
The embedding-augmented conditions use Trillsson embeddings for non-semantic speech representation \cite{shor2022trillsson} and a VoxProfile/Whisper stream based on Whisper representations and VoxProfile speech analysis \cite{radford2023whisper, feng2025vox}.

\section{Modeling and Evaluation}
\subsection{History-Aware CVAE}
The reported models are history-aware conditional variational autoencoders (CVAEs).
A transformer history encoder summarizes the padded history sequence using a learned CLS token, two transformer layers, four attention heads, hidden size 256, GELU activations, and a padding mask.
The current conditioning vector is projected separately and concatenated with the history summary.
The CVAE latent variable $z$ represents residual variability in response behavior that is not determined by the observed conversation context.
The encoder approximates $q_{\phi}(z \mid x_t,h_t^{(H)},y_t)$, and the decoder predicts a diagonal Gaussian distribution over $y_t$ from $(x_t,h_t^{(H)},z)$.
Training minimizes
\begin{dmath}
    \mathcal{L} =
    -\log p_{\theta}(y_t | x_t,h_t^{(H)},z)
    + \beta D_{\mathrm{KL}}\left(q_{\phi}(z | x_t,h_t^{(H)},y_t) || p(z)\right),
    \label{eq:cvae_loss}
\end{dmath}
where $p(z)$ is a standard normal prior and the latent dimension is 16.
All sweep runs use z-scored numeric inputs and targets, a learned diagonal decoder variance, Adam with learning rate 0.003, weight decay $10^{-5}$, batch size 1024, dropout 0.1, gradient clipping at 1.0, and a maximum of 100 epochs.
The KL weight $\beta$ is annealed from 0.001 to 0.05, and early stopping uses held-out development fit with patience 20 and minimum improvement $10^{-4}$.

\subsection{Model Conditions}
We evaluate 16 conditions formed by crossing four history windows, $H\in\{1,3,5,7\}$, with four information settings.
The interpretable-only setting uses measured speech and context features without learned speech embeddings.
The Trillsson setting adds the Trillsson embedding stream.
The VoxProfile/Whisper setting adds the VoxProfile/Whisper stream.
The dual-embedding setting adds both streams.
All numeric features and embedding dimensions are scaled using training-set statistics.

\subsection{Evaluation Axes}
Held-out natural-response fit is evaluated on the Seamless test set using target-averaged scaled MSE, target-averaged $R^2$, and C-index over the 12 response measurements.
These MSE, $R^2$, C-index, and likelihood values are computed from the same posterior conditional evaluation path and should be read as fit summaries rather than deployable forecasts.
External-probe separation is evaluated by applying the same preprocessing and model checkpoints to the TRACE-derived EmotiVoice examples and computing
\begin{equation}
    \Delta_{\mathrm{LL}} =
    \mathrm{LL}_{\mathrm{natural\ test}} -
    \mathrm{LL}_{\mathrm{EmotiVoice}},
\end{equation}
where larger values indicate lower model likelihood for the EmotiVoice probe than for held-out natural responses.
For the balanced score in Table~\ref{tab:clean_sweep}, we average two normalized ranks: the rank for natural-test MSE and the rank for $\Delta_{\mathrm{LL}}$.
This score is a descriptive rank summary, not a principled training objective or universal model-selection criterion; it identifies conditions that are not extreme on only one axis.

The current likelihood values are posterior fit scores, because the CVAE evaluation path uses $q_{\phi}(z \mid x_t,h_t^{(H)},y_t)$.
They should therefore be interpreted as held-out conditional fit and separation summaries, not as final deployable prior-predictive S2S scores.
A deployed prior-predictive scoring system should sample from $p(z)$ using only the conditioning turn and history, then compute predictive intervals and residuals for the candidate response.

\subsection{Fit-Separation Tradeoff}
The strongest separator is VoxProfile/Whisper with $H=7$, which gives the largest likelihood gap ($\Delta$LL 1.556) but the weakest natural-test MSE among the 16 conditions.
Conversely, the strongest natural-fit condition, interpretable-only $H=5$, gives the smallest MSE but a much smaller likelihood gap.
The balanced score is therefore useful only as a descriptive tradeoff summary: VoxProfile/Whisper $H=3$, Trillsson $H=1$, and dual embedding $H=7$ all receive score 0.800, but they occupy different parts of the tradeoff surface in Fig.~\ref{fig:tradeoff}.

The largest perceptual drops are in emotion flow, emphasis/stress, pauses/phrasing, and rhythm/timing.
Audio quality remains relatively high for EmotiVoice, especially after voice conversion, so the perceptual gap is not explained only by signal artifacts.
This study validates the EmotiVoice condition as an external synthetic probe that listeners perceive as less natural; it does not yet establish per-item correlation between our likelihood scores and human adjacent-turn judgments.

\section{Discussion}
The results point to two complementary roles for the feature families.
First, the strongest natural-response fit condition is not an embedding-heavy model; it is the interpretable-only $H=5$ condition.
This is encouraging because it means that directly measurable speech and context variables carry substantial information about response behavior in natural adjacent turns.
Second, the models that best separate TRACE-derived EmotiVoice examples from held-out natural examples do use learned speech embeddings.
This suggests that embeddings capture acoustic or paralinguistic mismatch that is not fully represented by the 12 interpretable response measurements.

These posterior-fit results support a two-axis view of S2S naturalness analysis.
If the goal is to fit the range of natural response measurements in this filtered setting, interpretable features with several turns of history are strongest.
If the goal is to screen against this external synthetic probe, embedding-augmented models provide stronger separation.
The balanced top tier should be interpreted as a descriptive set of practical choices rather than a universal winner: VoxProfile/Whisper $H=3$ offers the best MSE among the balanced models, Trillsson $H=1$ offers a compact short-history option, and dual embedding $H=7$ offers strong separation at the cost of weaker natural-set fit.

\section{Limitations}
Several limitations should be made explicit.
First, the reported likelihoods are posterior CVAE fit scores, not final prior-predictive S2S scores.
A deployed scoring system must score candidate responses using only the conditioning turn and history, without conditioning the latent variable on the response being evaluated.
Second, the TRACE-derived EmotiVoice probe is perceptually validated, but it represents one family of synthetic speech manipulations from a recent preprint, not the full space of S2S failure modes; separation may reflect both unnaturalness and source/pipeline mismatch.
Third, the listener study validates the external probe at the clip level; it does not yet establish per-item correlation between model scores and human judgments of adjacent-turn response naturalness.
Fourth, the filtering procedure focuses on substantive adjacent responses and excludes many backchannels, interruptions, long delays, and high-overlap turns.
Relatedly, response latency and overlap are treated as observed timing covariates in the current models, so these experiments do not evaluate unusual turn timing as a response failure.
Fifth, we use the source Seamless partitions directly and do not claim additional speaker- or session-disjoint resplitting beyond those partitions.
Finally, the target space is intentionally speech-native: it models measurable response behaviors, not semantic appropriateness, task success, factuality, user satisfaction, or naturalness as a direct human label.

\section{Conclusion}
We presented a conversation-conditioned framework for analyzing S2S response naturalness through distributions over interpretable speech behavior measurements.
The paper extends reference-based prosody and rhythm evaluation for spoken dialogue systems from static matched regimes to learned conditional response distributions, and uses TRACE-derived EmotiVoice examples as an external synthetic probe \cite{DISTRIB,TRACE}.
Using a 12-measure response space, interpretable metric and context features with a five-turn history give the strongest held-out natural posterior fit, while embedding-augmented models provide stronger natural-vs-probe separation.
Human ratings independently confirm that the EmotiVoice probe conditions are perceived as less natural than natural/reference speech, especially in emotion flow and speaking style.
Future work will convert the posterior fit scores into a prior-predictive scoring model and directly validate per-item scores against listener judgments.
